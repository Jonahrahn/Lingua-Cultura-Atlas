Methodology & sourcing

Research-oriented layers · illustrative cartography

What this atlas is

Selections combine linguistic typology (word order, morphology summaries, search keywords), religious-demographic summaries, and time-bounded territorial polities or cultural horizons. Figures (speaker counts, affinity scores) are pedagogical summaries, not live census data. ISO 639-3 codes name macro-languages where shown.

Territorial shading

Language-family and culture “washes” are hand-smoothed, illustrative spread shapes (regenerated from tools/generate-extent-geojson.py)—teaching schematics, not census, political, or belief maps. Their opacity tracks the main timeline: each wash is suppressed before that family’s or tradition’s conventional origin or founding date, then strengthens over a long schematic ramp (see in-app legend). Temporal territory polygons (History / Archaeology / Linguistic) are separately time-filtered by their own startYear–endYear ranges. The data layer bundles 130+ schematic extents, including deep prehistory and protohistory (continents, interaction spheres, and early states) plus teaching linguistics; you can merge more with tools/append-protohistory-territories.py. The timeline reaches about 50,000 BCE; resolution is coarse. Consult specialist work for boundary- or headcount-level claims.

References in-app

Detail panels list representative print and survey citations. Use them as entry points to the literature, not as exhaustive bibliographies.

Linguistic identifiers (research)

Where shown, ISO 639-3 codes identify macro-languages for bibliography and corpus work. Glottolog languoid IDs link to phylogenetic and bibliographic records (not the same as ISO scope). Use Grambank, PHOIBLE, and WALS through the links in the linguistic profile for typological comparison—always verify feature definitions against primary grammars.

History & encyclopedias (online)

Detail panels link to Wikipedia, Wikidata, Britannica, and (for some traditions) the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. These are tertiary summaries—convenient starting points, not citations for specialist claims. Cross-check dates, names, and boundaries against monographs and primary sources. Temporal territory popups include a Wikipedia search for the feature name plus any curated article URL stored in the data.

UNESCO World Heritage (points)

Markers show approximate anchor coordinates and official list numbers derived from a Wikidata query snapshot (tools/build-unesco-geojson.py), not a boundary GIS from UNESCO. For legal status, boundaries, and nomination text, use whc.unesco.org. The layer is clustered for performance; share links can store unesco=0|1 to turn it off or on.

General reference hubs

  • Wikipedia — overview articles and bibliographies
  • Wikidata — structured identifiers and sitelinks
  • Internet Archive — digitized books and historical media
  • Britannica — signed encyclopedia entries
  • World History Encyclopedia — illustrated articles
  • Library of Congress — collections and research guides

Good practice

  • Treat correlation sliders as heuristic, not causal proof.
  • Compare multiple independent sources before citing this UI in academic work.
  • For minority or endangered languages, prefer community and academic sources together.

Archaeology, symbolism & display

The atlas uses time-shaded territories, popups with optional Commons images, and a short symbolism blurb (material culture, art, ritual, and writing or graphic systems where it fits—still not a theology module). All photos are from Wikimedia—same licenses as Wikipedia.

How to use it

Turn on Archaeology and Linguistic in Territorial shading, move the timeline into the relevant range, and click the map. History, archaeology, and teaching-linguistics extents (Indus, Çatalhöyük, Latin epigraphy, Kufic Qurʾān, Maya glyphs, ogham stones, etc.) can show a lead image and a “Symbolism & display” block. Search also matches that symbolism text.

Material symbolism (themes)

Deep time: portable art, cave polychrome, and mammoth-ivory figurines as negotiable social knowledge—not always “religion” in a later sense. First villages & early states: seals, weights, and monument axes as legibility and seasonal liturgy. Imperial theatres: processional reliefs, kiva alignments, and baray-temples as public cosmology.
